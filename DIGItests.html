Digital tests for ELICOS

Standardized tests are useful for ranking, but not for learning.
— Sir Ken Robinson:

Problems

  • Adminstration Costs & Wastes: Schools rely on photocopied tests from textbooks or files, creating a printing cost and paper waste.
  • Classroom Disconnect: Teachers frequently adapt and personalise textbook content to better engage students, but assessments remain static and disconnected from classroom learning.
  • Students: They perceive paper-based tests as tedious and irrelevant, with little resemblence to the future high stake tests they will need to take to renew their visa.
  • Compliance: The tests seem like a task to tick off, rather than a tool for revision and independent learning.

Solution

  • English Australia EdTech Application – Learning Design Concept As part of my application for the English Australia EdTech initiative, I designed a flexible, teacher-centred assessment and delivery system that rethinks how classroom testing, marking, and reporting operate in real teaching environments. The concept focused on creating a single digital assessment framework that could be adapted by teachers to reflect what was actually taught in class, rather than forcing classes to conform to fixed tests. The system allowed teachers to log in and: Adapt and customise assessments in real time Deliver the same test across multiple contexts: printed, on student phones, or projected onto classroom boards Choose flexible response modes, enabling students to complete tasks digitally or on paper, depending on classroom needs Assessment data was captured automatically, allowing scores and reports to flow directly to administration systems, reducing duplication and manual handling. Learning Design Rationale & Benefits This approach was grounded in human-centred and practitioner-led design, prioritising the realities of classroom delivery. Key advantages included: Significant time savings for teachers and administrative staff Reduced paper usage, supporting more sustainable practices Lower operational costs through streamlined marking and reporting Improved alignment between teaching, assessment, and curriculum delivery Greater teacher autonomy, without increasing workload The project demonstrated how simple, adaptable digital systems can enhance assessment integrity while remaining practical, scalable, and responsive to real classroom constraints.

Research

Over the course of a year, this assessment format was tested across multiple ESL classes in several Sydney language schools. Student feedback was consistent and highlighted several clear advantages.

  • Higher engagement on phones
    Students overwhelmingly preferred the mobile version over paper.
  • Preferred hybrid mode
    Most liked questions on phones but answers on paper.
  • Low levels of cheating
    Cheating was rare; timers prevent AI-lookup.
  • Improved memory and recall
    Students said the format helped prompt their memory.
  • Voluntary revision outside class
    Many reviewed their feedback on public transport.

Solution

Design the assessment system around simple, modular templates that teachers can adapt instantly, without technical friction or platform lock-in.

  • 1. Flexible Delivery Modes
    • Mobile Q&A with automatic scoring
    • Phone questions + paper answer sheet
    • Projected test + phone responses
  • 2. Easy Teacher Editing and Localisation
    • Update questions to match what was taught
    • Personalise to city, students, or current events
    • Edit via Word → paste into template
  • 3. Consistency With Textbook Pedagogy

    • Write-from-dictation style tasks
    • Summaries, reorderings, gap-fills
  • 5. Time + Cost Savings
    • Templates ready to go each week

Problems

Most languages have 5 vowel sounds written and spoken, English has 5 written and around 25 spoken: as a result English as a second language students are confused about how to pronounce words and it will drag their score down in tests like Pearsons PTE

Pedagogy

Here is a sample of how this could be done at an intermediate level, but it could be graded down with the last activities dropped, or graded up with the first activity dropped.

  • 1. Listen and Watch
    • Listen and watch the sounds being made with examples
    • Can be done as a class or individually on students’ phones
  • 2. Play the Game
    • Listen to the words and sort them into four sounds on individual phones
    • Teacher may check in with students individually or drill the words as a class at any point
  • 3. Extension Activities
    • Write‑from‑dictation tasks
    • Gap‑fill activities
  • 4. Group Competition
    • Divide the class into groups and project the game on the board
    • Teams score points and lose points for correct and incorrect answers

Observations in the Classroom

  • Short, integrated tasks allowed students to work at their own pace, with faster students completing extension challenges that supported mixed‑level classes.
  • These shorter tasks encouraged self‑reflection and helped students take ownership of their learning rather than moving passively through activities.
  • Students maintained strong focus for approximately 15 minutes; early finishers discussed their learning individually before transitioning into group work once all students completed the task.
  • Clear, specific activities prompted genuine curiosity among students.
  • Students often forget concepts from one day to the next; keeping a record on their phones provided a reliable reference for later review.
  • Videos and PowerPoints without teacher interaction or embedded tasks have limited value. Like social media, students tend to watch passively and retain very little. For media to be effective, it must require active engagement.

Learning Through Music

For most of human history, music functioned as a memory technology. Long before writing existed, ancient civilisations used rhythm, melody, and repetition to preserve knowledge across generations. In Ancient Greece, epic poems such as The Iliad and The Odyssey were sung rather than read, because melody made thousands of lines easier to recall. In West Africa, griots carried genealogies, laws, and histories through musical storytelling. And in Australia, Indigenous cultures maintained vast networks of knowledge through songlines—musical maps encoding geography, law, spirituality, and survival skills. A songline wasn’t just a song; it was a living archive.

Even classical philosophers recognised the cognitive power of music. In Plato’s dialogue Phaedrus, Socrates warns that the new technology of writing would weaken human memory. He believed that once people relied on written text, they would stop exercising the mental discipline supported by rhythm, recitation, and song. Whether or not he was right, the anxiety reveals how deeply music was tied to memory and learning.

Modern research continues to show why music is so effective for learning. Writing lyrics, shaping rhythm, and choosing melody require deeper processing of vocabulary, structure, stress, and pronunciation. When students turn language into a song, they encode it across multiple neural pathways. The result is stronger recall, clearer pronunciation, and higher engagement than traditional drills.

Challenge Students

What we learn to do, we learn by doing .
— Aristotle

Problems

Many students plateau at around intermediate level of their language acquisition: held back by small grammar errors (often partly caused by boredom and a lack of attention), low confidence, and a cycle of repetition without retention. Teachers sometimes keep them on material that is unchallenging and uninteresting, I've seen teachers use materials made for native children on international adults. All this only reinforces the plateau, the feeling that they will never be fluent, instead of helping them move forward.

Assumed Knowledge

The students have seen all the past and present verb tenses but have not yet mastered them.

Mixed Language Points

  • Listening, Writing annd Speaking
  • Grammar: revision of all past and present verb forms
  • Guidance on how and when to use these verbs in biography writing
  • Optional extension activity to review verb structure if needed
  • Listening task based on an interview
  • Writing a biography or speaking about a well‑known person
  • Technology and business vocabulary

Krashen’s Input Hypothesis is a key theory in second language acquisition. It proposes that learners make the most progress when they are exposed to language that is understandable but slightly beyond their current level. This balance between familiarity and challenge creates ideal conditions for natural language growth and has strongly influenced modern approaches to reading, listening, and curriculum design.

  • It emphasises that learners acquire language through comprehensible input, not by memorising grammar rules in isolation.
  • It states that progress occurs when input is just above the learner’s current level (i+1), creating productive challenge.
  • It focuses on meaningful communication, where learners understand the message even if some language is new.
  • It supports using authentic materials, as long as they are scaffolded enough to remain understandable.
  • It highlights the power of extensive reading and listening for long‑term language development.
  • It underlines that low anxiety and high motivation help learners engage with slightly challenging input more effectively.

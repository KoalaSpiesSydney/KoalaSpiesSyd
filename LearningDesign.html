Problems

This is a problem I noticed while working in English Colleges in Australia: schools rely heavily on photocopied tests, creating unnecessary administration costs and waste; classrooms are tied to outdated textbooks that feel disconnected from real life in Australia; students, used to digital environments, find paper-based tests tedious and demotivating; and compliance-driven assessment practices often reduce testing to a box-ticking exercise rather than a meaningful measure of progress.

Research

Over the course of a year, this assessment format was tested across multiple ESL classes in several Sydney language schools. Student feedback was consistent and highlighted several clear advantages.

  • Higher engagement on phones
    Students overwhelmingly preferred the mobile version over paper.
  • Preferred hybrid mode
    Most liked questions on phones but answers on paper.
  • Low levels of cheating
    Cheating was rare; timers prevent AI-lookup.
  • Improved memory and recall
    Students said the format helped prompt their memory.
  • Voluntary revision outside class
    Many reviewed their feedback on public transport.

Solution

Design the assessment system around simple, modular templates that teachers can adapt instantly, without technical friction or platform lock-in.

  • 1. Flexible Delivery Modes
    • Mobile Q&A with automatic scoring
    • Phone questions + paper answer sheet
    • Projected test + phone responses
  • 2. Easy Teacher Editing and Localisation
    • Update questions to match what was taught
    • Personalise to city, students, or current events
    • Edit via Word → paste into template
  • 3. Consistency With Textbook Pedagogy
    • It is the same test but with updates
  • 4. High-Stakes Exam Alignment
    • Write-from-dictation style tasks
    • Summaries, reorderings, gap-fills
  • 5. Time + Cost Savings
    • Templates ready to go each week
    • No photocopying or subscription frees

A Case Study

This is an activity I did in an intermediate class last year.

  • Students wrote their own sentences and selected genres, then their ideas were transformed into full songs using Suno, another audio creating platform or if they have talent, play and record.
  • Grammar and pronunciation targets were uploaded into the system, which students found amusing but surprisingly effective for reinforcing accuracy.
  • The process encouraged collaboration — students compared lyrics, shared ideas, and refined each other’s language.
  • The students leave your class with a memory and an achievement, which can only be good for the teachers and schools word-of-moth.

Learning Through Music

For most of human history, music functioned as a memory technology. Long before writing existed, ancient civilisations used rhythm, melody, and repetition to preserve knowledge across generations. In Ancient Greece, epic poems such as The Iliad and The Odyssey were sung rather than read, because melody made thousands of lines easier to recall. In West Africa, griots carried genealogies, laws, and histories through musical storytelling. And in Australia, Indigenous cultures maintained vast networks of knowledge through songlines—musical maps encoding geography, law, spirituality, and survival skills. A songline wasn’t just a song; it was a living archive.

Even classical philosophers recognised the cognitive power of music. In Plato’s dialogue Phaedrus, Socrates warns that the new technology of writing would weaken human memory. He believed that once people relied on written text, they would stop exercising the mental discipline supported by rhythm, recitation, and song. Whether or not he was right, the anxiety reveals how deeply music was tied to memory and learning.

Modern research continues to show why music is so effective for learning. Writing lyrics, shaping rhythm, and choosing melody require deeper processing of vocabulary, structure, stress, and pronunciation. When students turn language into a song, they encode it across multiple neural pathways. The result is stronger recall, clearer pronunciation, and higher engagement than traditional drills.

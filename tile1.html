Digital Updatable Tests

Standardized tests are useful for ranking, but not for learning.
— Sir Ken Robinson:

Problems

  • Adminstration Costs & Wastes: Schools rely on photocopied tests from textbooks or files, creating a printing cost and paper waste.
  • Classroom Disconnect: Teachers frequently adapt and personalise textbook content to better engage students, but assessments remain static and disconnected from classroom learning.
  • Students: They perceive paper-based tests as tedious and irrelevant, with little resemblence to the future high stake tests they will need to take to renew their visa.
  • Compliance: The tests seem like a task to tick off, rather than a tool for revision and independent learning.

Solution

  • While researching and testing in a Sydney classroom over the course of a year, I trialled different combinations of delivery methods: test papers and answers delivered on phones; tests displayed on whiteboards or phones but answered on paper; tests with varied timers; and tests with hints and cues to make low‑stakes weekly progress checks more meaningful.
  • I used AI to rapidly code and develop tests covering different aspects of PTE, IELTS, and Cambridge, continually updating the content and using my human experience to personalise it to the grammar and vocabulary sets most relevant to the students.
  • Student feedback Students liked the combination of phone deliver and paper answer sheets. Many students also said that the test helped them recall and remember information more easily, and built confidence.
